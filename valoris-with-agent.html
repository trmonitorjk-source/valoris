The Challenge

Why traditional rewards analysis falls short

📊
Benchmarking without context
Market data shows where you rank — but not whether employees actually value what you're spending on, or how different groups feel differently about the same programs.
💸
Cost decisions made in isolation
Finance and HR operate with separate models. There's no single view connecting spend decisions to value outcomes — so ROI conversations are anecdotal, not quantified.
🎯
One-size-fits-all program design
Benefits are designed for the average employee. But executives, early-career ICs, and long-tenured managers have fundamentally different value hierarchies — and the data to show it is rarely surfaced.
📋
No audit trail for decisions
When a CHRO or CFO asks "why did we choose this design?" there's rarely a rigorous, reproducible analytical framework behind the answer. Decisions rely on instinct and consultant narrative.

Platform Guide & Methodology
How every chart works · How to explain it · The math behind the scores
②
Design Workbench — Frontier Chart
Market vs Value / Value vs Cost Δ / Market vs Cost Δ / Bubble
What it shows: Each dot is one program. The frontier line connects programs that are simultaneously best — no other program offers more value at lower cost. Programs on the frontier are "efficient." Programs below it are underperforming relative to spend.
How to read it: Current baseline = dashed blue line. Proposed selections = solid teal line. When the teal frontier shifts up and to the left, you're getting more value for less cost. Dots in the upper-right quadrant (Optimal) are high-performers. Lower-left = High Risk.
Client talking point
"The frontier shows which programs are pulling their weight. Any program below the line is costing more per unit of employee value than it should. These are your redesign candidates."
③
Multi-Dataset Analytics — Frontier Shift
Year-over-year · Client-over-client
What it shows: Same frontier visualization but layered across multiple datasets. When lines shift up-left over time, the program portfolio is becoming more efficient. Diverging lines between clients highlight competitive gaps.
How to read it: Each colored frontier = one dataset. Frontiers higher and further left are more efficient. Crossing frontiers = mixed performance across programs. The vs Prior Year toggle isolates a single client's trajectory.
Client talking point
"Since last year, your portfolio frontier has shifted upward — you're generating more employee value from roughly the same total investment. These three programs drove the improvement."
④
ROI Rankings — Value Points per $100K
Sortable by ROI, Cost, Market, Program
What it shows: For every upgrade alternative in the dataset, what does $100K of investment buy in value score points? Higher bars = better return. Programs with infinite ROI offer value gain at zero or negative cost.
How to read it: Sort buttons change the scoring dimension. Click the same sort button twice to reverse direction. Click any row to jump to that program in the Workbench and explore alternatives directly.
Client talking point
"If you have $500K to spend, the optimizer shows you can get the maximum value gain by prioritizing Flexible Work and L&D — not by defaulting to salary. The ROI on behavioral benefits is typically 3–5× higher."
④
Package Frontier — Scenario Comparison
Named packages as composite portfolio points
What it shows: Each named package (e.g. "Retention Focus") is collapsed into a single composite point representing its average value, average market score, and total cost delta. This lets you compare full scenarios at a glance.
How to read it: Packages further up and to the right are higher-performing. The frontier connects the efficient packages. In cost views, packages to the left offer the same or better performance at lower spend.
Client talking point
"We've built three named scenarios. The Retention Focus package scores highest on employee value at comparable cost to the status quo. The Growth Bet costs more but moves market position significantly — that's your call based on hiring pressure."
Calculation Methodology — Step by Step
Value Score
Definition: A 0–100 index of how much employees value this program, normalized across your workforce.

Source: Typically derived from employee surveys (e.g. "how important is this benefit to you?"), HR engagement data, or exit interview analysis.

In this platform: Entered directly per alternative. Values above 70 = high priority. Below 50 = low perceived value relative to cost.
Market Score
Definition: A 0–100 index of where this program sits relative to market — peer companies competing for the same talent.

Source: Benchmarking surveys (Mercer, WTW, Radford), proxy data, recruiter intelligence, or industry databases.

In this platform: Entered per alternative. 50 = market median. 75+ = top quartile. Below 40 = below market (retention risk).
ROI Score
Formula:
ROI = (ΔValue / CostDelta) × 100,000

Where: ΔValue = selected alt value − baseline value. CostDelta = spend above baseline ($0).

Interpretation: "Value points gained per $100K invested." ROI = ∞ means value gain at zero or negative cost. Sort the ROI table by different dimensions to stress-test assumptions.
Frontier Calculation
Algorithm: Sort all programs by X-axis value ascending. Walk left to right, keeping each point only if its Y value exceeds all previous Y values (Pareto-optimal set).

Result: The staircase line connecting the best programs — any program not on the frontier is dominated by a combination of cheaper and better-performing programs.
Cost Delta
Definition: The incremental cost vs. the status quo (baseline, $0).

Positive (+): This alternative costs more than current. E.g. upgrading from 4% to 5% 401k match.

Negative (–): This alternative saves money. E.g. moving to a high-deductible health plan.

Note: All absolute cost figures should be modeled per employee then scaled to headcount by the analyst.
Budget Optimizer
Algorithm: Greedy knapsack — sort all upgrade alternatives by ROI score descending, then greedily select the highest-ROI options until the budget is exhausted.

Limitation: Greedy knapsack is near-optimal but not guaranteed optimal for non-continuous item sizes. For CFO audit, present top 3–5 combinations for sensitivity analysis.
Quadrant Reference — Market vs Value Chart
✦ Optimal
High Value + High Market. These programs are both loved by employees AND competitive vs. peers. Protect them — cutting here destroys dual value.
↑ Investment
High Market + Low Value. Competitive externally but employees don't feel it. Explore redesign or communication strategy — the spend may be wasted.
⚠ Value Risk
High Value + Low Market. Employees love it but it's below market. Retention risk if competitors close the gap. Consider investing to also gain market position.
⚡ High Risk
Low Value + Low Market. Neither employees nor the market value this program. Redesign or reallocate spend — this is your first candidate for reduction.
